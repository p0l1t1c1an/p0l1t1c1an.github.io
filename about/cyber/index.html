Welcome to my rants about cyber security, computers, and network. This is really just some questions, ponderings, and ideas I have. My guess is that I don't know enough about the questions I have and would love to learn how wrong I am.

Consumer's should have access to security information, privacy information, source code, and hardware specs for tech products or online services

I think this could simply be thought as the nutritional info for the tech industry. Basically, just something "printed on the box" and easily accessible for most people but I would like an in-depth version available. This would have to be maintained by multiple large regulatory bodies, which review products like they do with food. For websites, this could be some form of requirement for something like PCI compliance. Then, a browser could notify a user whether the site is trusted/certified for X, Y, and Z, then it gives a ranking instead of simple a lock. So if you want be able take online transactions, you have to be open with consumers and certified "trusted" by X body. As well, I would like features should only be opt-in like sending crash reports and such.

I like to think of this as something like state and federal bar associations but for IT and cybersecurity professionals. Ultimately, I think we need such a mandatory association that can preform ethical reviews and preventing people working in industry. Then, we also could have it provide a system of training and certification. Really I think this is needed for many major "high-ticket" occupations as a way to ensure trust for both empolyers and customers.

Why are end user systems overly simple

It seems like everything you by is like "here is an app to control your device" and then its just a remote on/off switch. For peaks sake, my router had an almost useless app on it! It could just simply serve https on the LAN and be universally available. My main gripe is just that these devices want people to be stupid about what it is doing under the hood. I have also used configs that have "more info" that simple states "enable/disable X" instead of describing X to the user. Systems need to be highly configurable but also be in depth about what X does and any warnings with changing the default. Ultimately, users need to be able to learn what a device/system does and make it bend to their every whim. The device serves the user not the other way around!

To Copyleft, or to not Copyleft, that is the question

I don't really care about the licensing of software. I mearly care that what I use is free, and it upsets me that I have to use propritory firmware. However, both copyleft and permissive licenses have their ups and downs. Copyleft enforces that future developers that want to include the code into a larger project must have it copyleft licensed. Permissive licenses don't enforce such a thing allowing people to include the code into any project. This means that copyleft limits choice for a future developer and permissive fails to ensure freedom of the code. My opinion is that a library should be permissive and end-user software is copyleft. Though, I tend to just use BSD license since I don't care enough. Either way they are free thus able to be viewed and modified by a larger community.

Unsurprisingly, I don't use any "smart" home devices and actively despise them. They really just seem like unneeded risks with zero benefits. My main concern with them is punching holes through my router. There are many devices that connect through plug n' play just so a phone app can connect to it. These become easy ways onto my home network, so I can't trust them. That's why these devices must be isolated on a different VLAN if you're gonna use them. But then so can entire world. There's honestly a ton of issues with these kinds of devices like QA and security testing being costly and difficult as these are cyber-physical devices. You have to test software, hardware, network capabilites, and it physical interaction that end users will experience. It's very easy for things to slip by especially with tight time constraints. Plus, these devices seem like most of the time they are not designed. Rather a manager came up with an idea and gave 2 weeks to get back a product. No designing how the system should function or its features, just throw random tech together and you get a "smart" baby camera. Now ship it! What? Testing? Unnecessary! Now, everyone can watch your child sleep.

My biggest fear though may be once these devices make more money being "smart" than not. This is the case for most of modern cars, which basically house a general-purpose desktop inside them. That is happening a lot soon than people seem to think.

Backdoors: Why the Risk?

One of the things I fail to understand is the why governments seem to persistently decrease security. They want corporations to have backdoors in their encryption, ban E2EE, or pocket zero days. For a democratic nation like the US, this seems like more of a risk to their nations overall well being rather than the preventative measures will have on crime. Purposely creating flawed systems will be breached eventually and cost millions, billions of dollars. As well, smart criminals won't be affected by such backdoors and will have to be caught via (cyber) forensics. The zero days are more interesting. The militaries of nations that find security vulnerabilities can either report such flaws or use it against an adversary. I just don't think it is useful to pocket away vulnerabilities allow for other to discover and use such a vuln. It should be used and reported. Just no waiting to do something with them. That is a preventible disaster.
